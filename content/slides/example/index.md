## Blog 1: Introduction
At its heart, this project asks a simple but important question: can digital jobs be leveraged to challenge patriarchal norms in India?
We know that gender inequality remains a stubborn barrier to India’s social and economic progress. But we also know that millions of people—especially in underserved regions—are entering the digital economy. Through this project (supported by WAPP), I integrated light-touch content into the everyday digital tasks that workers at a large AI-ML data generation firm already complete. The goal was to test whether exposure to new ideas, embedded seamlessly into work, can shift attitudes toward gender equality.

## Blog 2: Building a Partnership with Karya
For this project, I partnered with Karya, a Bangalore-based pioneering digital labor platform that offers paid work opportunities to low-income communities across India. Their workers transcribe audio, record speech, and label images to support data collection in Indian languages.
Karya’s mission—to combine dignified livelihoods with social good—made them the ideal partner. This collaboration allows us to reach thousands of digital workers in an innovative way, embedding social research directly into a functioning platform that people already trust and rely on for income.

## Blog 3: Preparing for the Field – Designing the Intervention

Before the summer rollout, months of preparation went into designing intervention content and surveys. We worked to ensure that every question and prompt was culturally precise and sensitive.
For example, a survey question intended to measure whether men valued their wives’ opinions was at first misinterpreted as asking whether they needed their wives’ permission. This subtle but critical difference only became apparent during pre-testing. These iterations highlighted the importance of piloting and refining before scale.

## Blog 4: Learning from the Pilot in Ranchi

The pilot study, run in Ranchi, Jharkhand, provided invaluable insights. Workers tested the surveys and tasks on Karya’s mobile app, allowing us to observe firsthand where things worked and where adjustments were needed.
Some interventions that looked strong on paper proved impractical in practice, while others benefited from simple tweaks—like adding open-text response options so participants could clarify their thoughts. These lessons shaped the more ambitious rollout that would follow.

## Blog 5: Scaling Up with Karya

This summer was spent working closely with Karya’s engineering and research teams in Bangalore to move the project from pilot to large-scale rollout. The focus was on translating a complex intervention—previously tested with a small group in Ranchi—into a stable digital workflow that could support thousands of workers across multiple states.

Much of the work involved detailed coordination: refining survey logic, embedding intervention content into Karya’s mobile app, and running stress tests to ensure smooth performance under heavy user traffic. The collaboration highlighted how essential it is to bridge social science design with the realities of platform engineering. What began as a research idea in draft documents and spreadsheets was, by the end of the summer, integrated into a functioning system ready for scale.

## Blog 6: Bringing the Experiment to the Field
This summer the project was scaled across multiple districts in Uttar Pradesh, Bihar and Jharkhand (North India). These regions were chosen deliberately: they combine widespread smartphone use with some of the most entrenched patriarchal norms in India.

## Blog 7: Reflection 

This journey has been made possible by many hands: donors who believed in the vision, colleagues and advisors who shaped the design, and the team at Karya who translated theory into practice. Most of all, I owe thanks to the workers at Karya who participated with openness, patience, and generosity of spirit.
Development research is not just about data and results; it is about mutual respect and shared learning. That ethos has carried this project forward at every stage.

## Blog 8: Looking Ahead – Building Pathways for Change

As we move into the next phase of this study, the excitement lies not only in analyzing and disseminating findings but in the possibilities. Can digital platforms—already engines of economic opportunity—also become vehicles for reshaping harmful social norms?

If successful, this approach could scale at low cost across thousands of workers in India and beyond. Karya itself is expanding rapidly, with ambitions to reach new geographies like Kenya. Embedding social change into digital work is still an emerging idea, but one that could transform how we think about the intersection of livelihoods, technology, and equality.
